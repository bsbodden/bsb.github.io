The Topic of the Day

Fake news. You've heard that term before. You've likely even been recently exposed to fake news. Fake news affect public debate on the economy, the environment, healthcare, and it can directly affect the choices of politicians, voters, and the market. The Internet has made it possible to shift public perception in a way that was previously impossible. But have you ever stopped to think about fake news? How is it created, how does it spread, how does it impact you, and most importantly, what can you do about it?

Start From the Source

Fake news gain traction as they spread and change the perception of reality of those who believe it and further contribute to their spreading. However, in order for fake news to spread, someone has to create it to begin with.

But who? Let's use COVID-19 as an example.

A report by the Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) showed that only 12 people were responsible for 65% of COVID-19 related lies on social media. They were called The Misinformation Dozen

The Power of The Few

The massive followings and engagement of these twelve people allowed them to spread misinformation about the pandemic and question the effectiveness of vaccines, reaching millions.

We have managed to obtain the Twitter interactions for 6 of those people, the Misinformation 6, whose accounts have not been suspended. These individuals are still active on the platform.

We examined their interactions on Twitter, and what we found is it only takes as few as half a dozen people to create a deep network of lies

What is the point of fake news?

From climate change to vaccines, the Misinformation Six talk about topics of high socioeconomic relevance. The presence of agents who spread misinformation on social media disrupts public debate by making it hard to agree on the basic facts that ground a discussion.

With all that said, what is the point of it? Why would someone dedicate time and resources just to spread falsehoods?

But spreaders of misinformation don't just lie for the sake of it. For every lie they spread, there is a goal to achieve, and a narrative to tell.

The Oldest Trick in the Book

From everything you've seen so far, you may believe that spreading fake news is a recent phenomenon, a consequence of technology enabling people to reach the entire world instantly. While it is true that the term "fake news" only became current around 2016, fraudsters have been aware of the power of misinformation since long before that. While the Internet has allowed the dissemination of fake news on an unprecedented scale, fake news is a tale as old as time..

Tale as old as time, fake as it can be

Why Does This Matter?

So, even though fake news has gained a surge in recent years, it is actually older than sliced bread. So what's this buzz about misinformation now? Why should I care? If in 1693 one man with his printer in a basement in London could pose a threat against the king, and one fake letter in 1803 caused fortunes to be made and destroyed in stock exchanges, imagine the devastation that fake news powered by the internet could cause. Or don't imagine, you don't have to. Just look at COVID-19. Misinformation is used for personal profit. Misinformation shakes the trust in our institutions. Misinformation kills.

This interactive visualization allows you to explore the activities of the "Disinformation Half-Dozen", 6 of the 12 people responsive for 65% of disinformation about COVID-19 who still have active Twitter accounts. It allows you to use the dropdown box to select:

  • By # of Tweets - The total number of times the person posted on Twitter. You can notice there's a large burst of this activity before the major COVID-19 spike in January, 2021.
  • By # of Retweets - The total number of times the person was retweeted, which is when another person reposts their message directly. If the person retweeting has more followers than the original poster, this can strongly amplify the reach of the message.
  • By # of Favorites - The total number of times the person received a "like." This is an indication of how many people read and reacted to their posts.
